Australian Bushfires and a Changing Climate


The 2019/20 Australian Bushfires have been the most devastating bushfires in Australia's history.




The substantial and long-lasting fires are the largest in magnitude in the modern record in New South Wales, while the total area burnt appears to be the largest in a single recorded fire season for eastern Australia

How the Bushfires Affect our Communities

Of the 9 firefighters that passed away trying to prevent the fires 5 of those were American firefighters Captain Ian McBeth, First Officer Paul Hudson and Flight Engineer Rick DeMorgan Jr who were killed when their water-bombing C-130 Hercules aircraft operated by Coulson Aviation crashed in southern NSW on 23 January 2020.


In addition to the estimated 17 million hectares of land lost due to the fires it is believed that up to 3094 houses have been destroyed.

How the Bushfires Affect our Wildlife


The bushfires have threatened many species of Australian wildlife.




Estimates, based on NSW and Victoria only, project losses of over one billion mammals, birds and reptiles combined. This excludes insects, the loss of which has been reported to be in the ‘hundreds of billions’.

How Australia's Climate is Changing

Australia's climate has warmed just over 1 °C since 1910 leading to an increase in the frequency of extreme heat events.




There has been a long-term increase in extreme fire weather, and in the length of the fire season, across large parts of Australia.




The Bureau of Meterology (BOM) expects further increases in sea and air temperatures, with more hot days and marine heatwaves, and fewer cool extremes.

How Australia is Contributing to its Climate




Despite the threat increased temperatures has on Australia's landscape, wildlife and people, Australia is still increasing CO2 emissions.

How Australia Compares to the Globe



In 2015, the total electricity consumption in Iceland was 18,798 GWh. Renewable energy provided almost 100% of electricity production, with about 73% coming from hydropower and 27% from geothermal power.




Iceland has demonstrated that you can support a stable economy with renewable electricity. Australia has an opportunity to follow Iceland's footsteps and protect its future landscape, economy and wildlife.
